## SEGMENT 1 — Before the British Arrived: Clam-Land and the Mi'kmaq

[HOST] [paper rustling] Before a single European settler hammered a nail into Lunenburg's famous hillside, this land had a name. The Mi'kmaq people called it **āseedĭk** — meaning, simply, *clam-land.* When the Acadians arrived and tried to pronounce the Mi'kmaq name, it morphed into *Mirliguèche* — or sometimes *Merligash* — and that's what it was called on French maps for generations.

[HOST] The Mi'kmaq had lived along this coastline for thousands of years — fishing, hunting, and trading across a vast territory they called *Mi'kma'ki.* The land around what would become Lunenburg was rich in marine life, sheltered harbours, and dense forest. It was, to put it plainly, a spectacular place to build a life.

[HOST] [sighs] And then — as you might expect from the mid-18th century — the British showed up.

[HOST] By 1749, Britain's grip on Nova Scotia was precarious. The colony was dominated almost entirely by the Mi'kmaq — who had never surrendered their sovereignty — and by roughly ten thousand French-speaking, Roman Catholic *Acadians* who had lived here for well over a century. The British Board of Trade had a blunt solution: flood the region with Protestant settlers. Dilute the French Catholic population. Cement British control.

[HOST] [curious] So they launched an aggressive recruitment campaign across Europe — targeting the German Palatinate, the Swiss cantons, and the French-speaking principality of Montbéliard. These were people fleeing religious persecution, crushing taxes, and desperately overcrowded homelands. The British offered them land, food, tools, and passage. It seemed like a deal too good to refuse.

[HOST] It wasn't too good to be true — but it was *complicated.* Very complicated.

*[PAUSE]*

---

## SEGMENT 2 — 1753: The Founding That Almost Wasn't

[HOST] [excited] On June 8th, 1753, a small flotilla of ships nosed into the harbour at Mirliguèche. Onboard were **1,453 settlers** — mostly German-speaking Protestants — along with 150 British troops and militia. The man responsible for laying out the new town was a Boston-born surveyor named **Charles Morris,** who carved a tidy rectangular grid into the hillside — the same grid you can walk today.

[HOST] The town was renamed **Lunenburg** — in honour of King George II, who also happened to be Duke of *Braunschweig-Lünenburg,* a principality back in Germany. A neat political move. Call it "we named it after your homeland" diplomacy.

[HOST] [laughs] Now — these settlers were farmers. From landlocked regions of Europe. Dropped onto the edge of the North Atlantic. With thin, rocky soil that resisted cultivation, surrounded by thick forest, and with no established supply chain. This was not the promised land they had been sold on.

[HOST] [clears throat] Conditions deteriorated quickly. Rations were short. Promises from the British Parliament — about ongoing support, tools, land grants — were being broken or delayed. Rumours swirled through the settlement that the Crown was pocketing funds meant for the colonists.

[HOST] And in December of 1753 — just six months after arriving — the settlers *snapped.*

[HOST] [gasps] Led by a man named **John Hoffman** — a former Justice of the Peace from Halifax — an armed mob rose up against British authority. They locked the local Justice of the Peace and several of Commander Patrick Sutherland's troops inside a blockhouse. Then, in what might be the most audacious act in Lunenburg's early history — they declared a **republic.**

[HOST] [playfully] Yes. A republic. In 1753. On a foggy hill in Nova Scotia. The Lunenburg Rebellion, also called the Hoffman Insurrection, was short-lived — British forces quickly reasserted control, Hoffman was arrested, and the uprising was quashed. But it reveals something crucial: these settlers were not passive. They were proud, principled, and *not* to be pushed around.

## SEGMENT 3 — War, Raiders, and a Town Under Siege

[HOST] [calm] The rebellion wasn't the only violence Lunenburg would face. The town was carved out of a landscape that was very much *contested territory.* The Mi'kmaq and the Acadians had a deep military and spiritual alliance — and they viewed the Protestant settlers on Mi'kmaw land as exactly what they were: agents of British colonial expansion.

[HOST] Over the following years, Lunenburg was attacked **nine times** by combined Mi'kmaq and Maliseet raiding parties. The 1756 raid was particularly devastating. Homes were burned. Livestock taken. The settlers — who had crossed an ocean for a new life — found themselves in the middle of a vicious colonial war they were barely equipped to survive.

[HOST] [sighs] Hostilities with the Mi'kmaq didn't fully end until around 1760. And just as peace arrived on that front — the American Revolution brought a new threat from the sea.

[HOST] [excited] On the morning of July 1st, 1782 — while Britain and her American colonies were still at war — five privateer vessels under a Massachusetts captain named **Noah Stoddard** slipped into Lunenburg harbour. These were state-licensed pirates — privateers — sailing under American letters of marque.

[HOST] Stoddard landed two miles from town, marched his 60-man crew through the dark, and issued a simple ultimatum: *surrender or burn.* The militia surrendered. The privateers burned the blockhouse anyway. They spiked the cannons so they couldn't be used. They raided the warehouses — helping themselves to generous quantities of rum. Then, in what is surely history's most theatrical act of looting, they *dressed up in the stolen scarlet uniforms of the British soldiers* and marched through town collecting everything of value.

[HOST] [laughs] You cannot make this up. Lunenburg was ransacked by drunk Americans in British costumes. The things they don't teach you in school.

*[PAUSE]*

## SEGMENT 4 — From Farmers to Fishermen: The Making of a Maritime Culture

[HOST] Despite war, rebellion, and piracy, Lunenburg *survived.* And what happened next is one of the most remarkable cultural transformations in Canadian history.

[HOST] [curious] Within two generations, those German and Swiss farmers — people whose ancestors had never seen the open ocean — became some of the *finest deep-sea fishermen in the world.* The poor soil of the South Shore pushed them toward the sea. And the sea rewarded them.

[HOST] Lunenburg became a world-class fishing port. Its shipyards launched schooner after schooner — sleek, powerful vessels that pushed out to the **Grand Banks,** one of the richest fishing grounds on earth. The Smith and Rhuland Shipyard became legendary. The catch was enormous: cod, halibut, haddock — salted, dried, and shipped around the Atlantic world.

[HOST] And then, in 1921, Lunenburg launched a ship that would become the most famous vessel in Canadian history.

[HOST] [excited] On March 26th, 1921, the **Bluenose** slid down the ways at Smith and Rhuland. She was designed by William Roué and built as both a working fishing schooner and a racing vessel — meant to reclaim a trophy that Nova Scotia had lost to an American schooner. Cost: $35,000, funded largely by the sale of shares at $100 each.

[HOST] She exceeded every expectation. The Bluenose was *undefeated* in international competition for **seventeen straight years.** Every fall she'd come in from the Grand Banks, scrub the salt off her hull, and go race — and win. Her image has graced the Canadian **dime** since 1937. She is not just Lunenburg's symbol — she is Nova Scotia's soul.

[HOST] [sighs] The original Bluenose was sold during the Second World War to haul freight in the Caribbean. In 1946, she struck a reef off Haiti and sank. A loss that still stings in Lunenburg. In 1963, Bluenose II was built — by many of the same craftsmen — at the same shipyard. She sails today as a floating ambassador for Nova Scotia.

## SEGMENT 5 — A World Heritage Town: Freezing History in Colour

[HOST] [calm] By the late 20th century, something remarkable had happened to Lunenburg. While countless other colonial-era towns across North America had been demolished, modernized, or simply left to rot — Lunenburg had *held on.*

[HOST] The original street grid drawn by Charles Morris in 1753 was still intact. Hundreds of wooden buildings from the 18th and 19th centuries still stood — colourfully painted, lovingly maintained, in styles that historians call **Lunenburg Bump** architecture — a distinctive local take on Colonial and Georgian design, characterized by the five-sided dormer windows that jut from rooflines across the old town.

[HOST] In 1992, the federal government designated Old Town Lunenburg a **National Historic District.** Three years later, in 1995, **UNESCO** added it to the World Heritage List — calling it the best surviving example of a *planned British colonial settlement* in North America.

[HOST] [excited] Of roughly 400 major buildings in the Old Town, fully **70 percent** date from the 18th or 19th centuries. This is not a reconstructed heritage site or a museum piece. People live here. They paint their houses in those famous jewel tones — ochre and crimson and seafoam green. They mend their boats and drink their coffee and grumble about the tourists — just as their great-great-grandparents might have done.

[HOST] [playfully] The tourists, for the record, are worth the grumbling. Lunenburg draws visitors from around the world who come to walk those historic streets, visit the Fisheries Museum of the Atlantic, and photograph that skyline.

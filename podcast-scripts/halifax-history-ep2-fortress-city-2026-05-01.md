## SEGMENT 1 — Why Here? The Logic of Halifax

[HOST] [paper rustling] To understand Halifax, you have to understand the strategic logic that brought it into existence. Because this was not a settler colony in any traditional sense. There was no particular gold rush, no particular religious exile driving it. Halifax was founded as a military-strategic counter-move.

[HOST] Here is the problem the British were solving. France had Louisbourg. Louisbourg was a massive, heavily fortified port on the eastern tip of Cape Breton Island — just a few hundred kilometres northeast of where Halifax now sits. It guarded the entrance to the St. Lawrence River, the main route into the interior of French Canada. It was one of the most expensive fortifications ever built in North America, and it was very, very good at what it did, which was to project French naval power across the North Atlantic fisheries and threaten British shipping.

[HOST] [curious] The British needed a counter. They needed a fortified port of their own on the Atlantic coast of Nova Scotia — one that could house a fleet, supply it, repair it, and serve as the military and administrative capital of a province they had been claiming since 1710 but had never really controlled. They needed, in other words, Halifax.

[HOST] Parliament voted forty thousand pounds for the project — a substantial sum — and appointed Edward Cornwallis as the governor of Nova Scotia and the man to make it happen. Cornwallis was forty years old, a veteran of the brutal Jacobite suppression in Scotland after the 1745 uprising. He knew how to impose order. He knew how to build. And he knew that what he was founding was not simply a town but a strategic instrument of imperial policy.

[HOST] When his fleet entered the harbour on that June morning, he named it Halifax — after the Earl of Halifax, president of the Board of Trade and Plantations, who had championed the project in London. Then he put his 2,500 settlers to work.

## SEGMENT 2 — Building from Scratch

[HOST] [calm] The speed at which Halifax went from empty hillside to functioning colonial capital is remarkable. Within days of landing, trees were being felled, streets were being laid out in a grid pattern — that original grid still forms the bones of the downtown — and a palisade wall was going up around the perimeter. The settlers worked because they had to. Winter was coming.

[HOST] They built fast and they built rough. The first structures were little more than timber shelters. Disease moved through the settlement quickly — scurvy, typhus, the general miseries of crowded colonial life. A significant number of those original 2,500 settlers did not survive the first year. By the end of the decade, waves of replacement settlers had been brought in — German Protestant settlers, called the Foreign Protestants, who founded the town of Lunenburg further down the coast in 1753 and became the agricultural backbone of the colony.

[HOST] [sighs] But the most immediate problem was not disease or winter. It was the Mi'kmaq.

[HOST] We talked in the last episode about Cornwallis's 1749 Scalp Proclamation — the bounties offered for Mi'kmaq scalps. It bears dwelling on for a moment, because it tells you something important about the logic of colonialism in this period. The Mi'kmaq were raiding British settlements, burning outlying farms, killing settlers who wandered too far from the palisade. From their perspective, they had been given no say in whether this city would be built on their land, and they were watching it rise anyway. From Cornwallis's perspective, the raids were an obstacle to be removed.

[HOST] The proclamation was not an aberration. It was policy. The British had used similar measures in Scotland, in Ireland. The idea was to make the cost of resistance so catastrophically high that resistance would stop. It didn't work in Nova Scotia the way Cornwallis hoped. The Mi'kmaq kept raiding. Cornwallis eventually left — replaced as governor in 1752 — but the Scalp Proclamation remained on the books for years.

[HOST] [curious] Halifax grew anyway. By the mid-1750s it had a newspaper — the *Halifax Gazette*, founded in 1752, the first newspaper published in what is now Canada. It had a proper legislature — Canada's first elected assembly met in Halifax in 1758. It had taverns, churches, a growing merchant class. It had the bones of something permanent.

[HOST] And then the war it had been built to fight finally arrived.

## SEGMENT 3 — The Seven Years' War: Halifax's Moment

[HOST] [paper rustling] The Seven Years' War is one of those conflicts that gets underplayed in North American history classes. Winston Churchill called it the first world war — it was fought on five continents, involved every major European power, and its outcome reshaped the world in ways we are still living with. In North America, it was the war that decided whether the continent would be British or French.

[HOST] And Halifax was the hinge on which that decision turned.

[HOST] The war in North America is usually dated from 1754, though it escalated dramatically after the formal European declarations in 1756. British strategy was straightforward: strangle French Canada by cutting it off from the sea. That meant taking Louisbourg. And taking Louisbourg meant using Halifax as the staging base.

[HOST] In 1758, a British force of roughly 12,000 soldiers and 10,000 sailors — an enormous armada for the period — assembled in Halifax harbour before sailing northeast to Cape Breton. The siege of Louisbourg lasted seven weeks. When it fell on July 26th, 1758, the St. Lawrence was open. The following year, General James Wolfe led his army up the river to Quebec. On September 13th, 1759, on the Plains of Abraham outside Quebec City, the war in Canada was effectively decided in a battle that lasted less than fifteen minutes. Both Wolfe and the French commander Montcalm died of their wounds.

[HOST] [calm] New France was finished. The Treaty of Paris in 1763 transferred almost all of France's North American possessions to Britain. The continent was now, overwhelmingly, British. And Halifax — the city that had been founded specifically to make this possible — now found itself at the centre of a changed world.

[HOST] There was a dark coda to the victory in Halifax's immediate vicinity. The fall of Louisbourg in 1758 brought the final wave of Acadian deportations — the communities that had survived or hidden from the 1755 expulsion were now hunted down systematically. The British burned every French settlement they could find on the Atlantic coast of Nova Scotia. The land was swept clean.

[HOST] [curious] It is a strange thing to reckon with — that a city founded in 1749 became, within a decade of its founding, the administrative centre from which the British completed one of the largest forced population removals in North American colonial history. Halifax did not exist before 1749. By 1764, the culture that had lived on this land for 150 years before the British arrived had been shattered, scattered, or suppressed.

[HOST] What came next was something new, built on that scarred ground.

## SEGMENT 4 — After the War: A Capital Takes Shape

[HOST] [clears throat] In the decades after the Seven Years' War, Halifax consolidated its role as the military and commercial capital of British North America's Atlantic coast. The harbour hummed with naval traffic. The Citadel — the star-shaped fortress that still crowns the hill above the downtown — was expanded and rebuilt multiple times through the late 1700s and early 1800s. The British understood that whatever else happened on this continent, they needed Halifax.

[HOST] The American Revolution accelerated everything. When the thirteen colonies to the south declared independence in 1776, Halifax became, almost overnight, the anchor of what remained of British North America. It was the primary naval base from which the British conducted operations throughout the war. And when the war ended in 1783 with American independence, something unexpected happened to Halifax.

[HOST] [calm] The Loyalists arrived. Tens of thousands of colonists who had remained loyal to the Crown — who had backed the wrong side, as far as the new United States was concerned — were expelled or fled from their homes. Roughly 35,000 of them came to Nova Scotia. Entire communities materialized seemingly overnight. The population of Nova Scotia nearly doubled in the space of a few years.

[HOST] These were not rough settlers. Many were educated, prosperous, connected. They brought skills, capital, and an intense desire to prove that a British North America could succeed — that everything they had sacrificed their former lives for had been worth it. They also brought deep prejudices and social hierarchies that would shape Nova Scotia for generations.

[HOST] Halifax at the end of the 18th century was a city of striking contrasts. Military grandeur and waterfront squalor. Merchant wealth and grinding poverty. A growing free Black community — many of them former American slaves who had been promised freedom in exchange for fighting for the British — living in settlements outside the city without the rights and land that had been promised to them.

[HOST] [sighs] The promises made and broken to Black Nova Scotians in these years are a thread that runs all the way through this history, and we'll follow it in a later episode to its most painful expression.

[HOST] But for now, let's note what Halifax had become by 1800: a fortified port city at the edge of the Atlantic, tested by war, shaped by waves of dispossessed humanity, and about to enter the most prosperous and swashbuckling period of its history.

[HOST] [curious] Because war, as Halifax was about to discover, could be very good for business.

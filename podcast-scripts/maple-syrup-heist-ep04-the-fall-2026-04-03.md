## Segment 1: The Arrests

[HOST] December 2012. Four-thirty in the morning. Temperature: minus eighteen Celsius. Three hundred officers across sixteen locations in a coordinated dawn raid. Twenty-two suspects. Every door hit simultaneously. The Sureté du Québec does not mess around.

[VOICE:dave] They also do not sleep in.

[HOST] They do not. The operation was planned down to the minute. Officers had been briefed the night before. Some of them, I am told, had difficulty maintaining composure during the briefing. The phrase "strategic maple syrup reserve" does that to people who haven't spent three months on the case.

Now, each arrest had its own... character. Let's start with the ringleader. Jean-Pierre Bouchard was found in his kitchen. In his underwear. Making pancakes.

[VOICE:dave] You cannot make this up.

[HOST] He was using stolen syrup.

[VOICE:dave] You absolutely cannot make this up.

[HOST] Officers entered through the front door. JP was standing at the stove, spatula in hand. There were forty barrels of stolen maple syrup in his garage. He was calm. He offered the officers breakfast. He asked if he could finish his pancakes. Both requests were declined.

[VOICE:josh] I told the officers, "You're arresting the wrong people." The officer said, "Sir, you're in your underwear and there are forty barrels of stolen syrup in your garage." I said, "Allegedly stolen." [clears throat] My lawyer told me to stop talking. I should have listened to my lawyer sooner. Like, eighteen months sooner.

[HOST] The officers did note, in their report, that the pancakes smelled excellent.

[VOICE:dave] Professionally relevant detail.

[HOST] Next. Luc Desmarais. The inside man. Arrested at the warehouse. Or rather, in the parking lot of the warehouse. Sleeping in his car.

[VOICE:adam] I knew it was coming. Every time I saw a police car, my heart stopped. Every time the phone rang. Every time someone knocked on my door. For a year, I didn't sleep. I stayed in the car because it was more peaceful than going home to my wife's questions. When they finally arrested me, I felt... relieved. [sighs] I slept eight hours that night. First time in a year. In a holding cell. On a metal bench. Best sleep of my life.

[HOST] His wife later told reporters she was also relieved. Though for different reasons. Luc, it should be noted, was found with a half-eaten bag of gas station chips, a blanket from home, and a resignation letter to the federation that he had written but never sent. It was dated three months prior.

[VOICE:dave] Marriage is complicated. So is quitting a crime ring, apparently.

[HOST] Then there was Big Mike Michaud. Pulled over on the Trans-Canada Highway at six in the morning. Hauling a flatbed truck with fourteen barrels of stolen syrup. Officers also found a handgun, forty thousand dollars in cash, and a copy of The Art of War by Sun Tzu. With margin notes. About supply chain management.

[VOICE:arnold] I told them it wasn't my truck. They pointed out it had my name on the door. In custom lettering. [PAUSE] Look, I panicked. It was early. I hadn't had my coffee. A man says stupid things before coffee.

[HOST] Big Mike's margin notes, by the way, included the annotation "Sun Tzu never had to deal with weigh stations" next to a passage about knowing your terrain. The officers photographed every page.

[VOICE:dave] Evidence. And also a pretty good read, apparently.

[HOST] And then there was cousin Raymond. Remember Raymond? The barber's brother-in-law who couldn't keep a secret?

[VOICE:dave] Cracked in eleven minutes.

[HOST] Raymond was already in custody. Had been cooperating for weeks. He was, in fact, the reason several of those sixteen locations were on the list at all. Raymond had provided names, addresses, phone numbers, and a hand-drawn map of the entire distribution network. In colored pencil.

[VOICE:dave] Thorough.

[HOST] Thorough. When JP learned that Raymond had been cooperating, he reportedly said one word. Just one. Which we cannot repeat on this podcast.

[VOICE:dave] It rhymes with "duck."

[HOST] It does. Raymond's colored pencil map, incidentally, was entered into evidence. It included a legend. And a compass rose. The prosecutor called it "the most helpful piece of criminal evidence I have ever received."

[VOICE:dave] Raymond missed his calling. Cartography. Not crime.

[HOST] By noon on that December morning, all twenty-two suspects were in custody. The Great Canadian Maple Syrup Heist was officially over. The cleanup, however, was just beginning.

## Segment 2: The Press Conference and Trial

[HOST] January 2013. Sergeant Diane Pelletier stood behind a podium at Sureté du Québec headquarters in Montreal. Thirty-seven journalists. Fourteen cameras. And one deeply surreal press conference.

[VOICE:sarah] I had to stand in front of those cameras and say, with a straight face, "We have recovered a portion of Canada's strategic maple syrup reserve." [PAUSE] The journalists laughed. I did not. I cited the figure. Eighteen point seven million dollars in stolen maple syrup. Three thousand tons. The laughter stopped. Mostly.

[HOST] A reporter from the BBC asked if this was a joke. Sergeant Pelletier cited the eighteen point seven million dollar figure a second time. Slowly.

[VOICE:sarah] He did not ask follow-up questions.

[HOST] A CNN correspondent asked how syrup could possibly be worth that much. Pelletier asked him how much he paid for extra virgin olive oil. He said he didn't know. She said, "Exactly. Now imagine someone stole all of it."

[VOICE:dave] International journalists could not believe this was real. To be fair, neither could most Canadians.

[HOST] The press conference made global headlines. Le Monde ran it on page three. The New York Times called it "the most Canadian crime imaginable." A headline in Australia read, "Sticky-Fingered Canadians Nabbed in Syrup Sting." Which, honestly, is excellent journalism.

[VOICE:dave] Pulitzer-worthy.

[HOST] The trial began in 2015. And it was, by all accounts, extraordinary. Twenty-two defendants. A courtroom that smelled faintly of maple, according to one reporter, though that may have been psychosomatic.

Jean-Pierre Bouchard. Sentenced to eight years in prison. Fined nine point four million dollars. If the fine went unpaid, an additional six years.

[VOICE:josh] Eight years. For syrup. I know people who got less for armed robbery. [PAUSE] My lawyer said I should express remorse. I'm remorseful that I got caught. Is that the same thing?

[HOST] It is not. Luc Desmarais received five years and a one point two million dollar fine. He cried in the courtroom. His mother cried in the gallery. His lawyer looked like he wanted to cry but was billing by the hour, so he maintained composure.

Big Mike Michaud. Two years less a day. His lawyer argued, with a straight face, that Big Mike genuinely believed he was transporting "artisanal water" for the first three months. The judge was unconvinced. But the sentence suggests mild entertainment.

[VOICE:dave] Artisanal water. In barrels. That smell like maple. Weighing six hundred pounds each.

[HOST] The defense was creative, if nothing else. In total, twenty-two people convicted. Combined sentences exceeding fifty years. Combined fines exceeding fifteen million dollars. And yet. Only twenty-five percent of the stolen syrup was ever recovered.

[VOICE:dave] The remaining seventy-five percent is out there. On someone's pancakes. In someone's oatmeal. Drizzled on someone's yogurt. It could be in your pantry right now. You'd never know.

[HOST] You genuinely would not. Stolen maple syrup is chemically identical to legal maple syrup. There is no test. No marker. No serial number on a pancake. Somewhere in Vermont, right now, someone is pouring stolen Canadian maple syrup on their French toast. They have no idea. They will never have any idea. And it tastes exactly the same.

[VOICE:dave] The perfect crime. Except for the getting caught part.

[PAUSE]

## Segment 3: The Supreme Court and Aftermath

[HOST] Now, Jean-Pierre Bouchard was not a man who accepted defeat quietly. He appealed. Specifically, he appealed the nine point four million dollar fine. His argument was remarkable in its audacity.

[VOICE:josh] The federation controls the price. They tell producers how much they can sell, who they can sell to, how much they can produce. That's not a cooperative. That's a cartel. I just... redistributed. You can't steal from thieves. That's not stealing. That's justice.

[HOST] The lower courts were partially sympathetic. In 2020, the fine was reduced to one million dollars. The federation was furious. They appealed. And this is how the Great Canadian Maple Syrup Heist ended up before the Supreme Court of Canada. In 2022, the Supreme Court reinstated the full nine point four million dollar fine.

[VOICE:dave] The Supreme Court of Canada ruled on maple syrup. This is the timeline we live in.

[HOST] The ruling was unanimous. Nine justices. Nine to zero. The written decision ran forty-three pages. Forty-three pages of the highest court in Canada meticulously explaining why you cannot steal maple syrup, even if you think the people storing it are charging too much. JP was philosophical about it.

[VOICE:josh] The Supreme Court of Canada knows my name. How many people can say that? I'm in the law books now. Forever. [PAUSE] My mother would be proud. If she wasn't so angry.

[HOST] As for the federation. They learned their lesson. Sort of. Their response to the largest agricultural theft in Canadian history was not to question the system that created a target worth stealing. No. They added more syrup to the reserve. Expanded capacity. Installed cameras, alarm systems, motion sensors, and electronic monitoring on every barrel.

[VOICE:dave] They hired more guards. Steve got a promotion.

[HOST] Steve got a promotion?

[VOICE:dave] Steve is now head of security. He has a badge and everything. A real one. Not laminated.

[HOST] The strategic reserve today holds over one hundred million pounds of maple syrup. Worth approximately half a billion dollars. Stored in a facility that now has, I am told, actual security infrastructure. The padlock has been replaced.

[VOICE:dave] Progress.

[HOST] The federation's president, Colette Fontaine, addressed the changes at a press conference in 2023.

[VOICE:rachel] We have invested significantly in security. The reserve is monitored twenty-four hours a day. We have learned from this experience. [PAUSE] And no, I will not be taking questions about Steve.

[HOST] Henri Laroche, the competition winner whose prize-winning syrup was stolen and mixed into the general haul, never did get his syrup back. He entered the competition again in 2014. He won second place. He has not spoken publicly about the heist. But his entry form that year listed his syrup's name as "Revenge." He did not elaborate.

[VOICE:dave] Henri understood the assignment.

[PAUSE]

## Outro

[HOST] The Great Canadian Maple Syrup Heist remains one of the most unusual crimes in modern history. Eighteen point seven million dollars. Three thousand tons. Twenty-two convictions. One Supreme Court ruling. And a strategic reserve that now has better locks. It exposed the strange reality of Canada's maple syrup cartel — a legal monopoly controlling a breakfast condiment with the intensity of a petroleum state. The OPEC of pancakes, as we called it in episode one. Still operational. Still powerful. Still storing syrup in a warehouse like it's gold bullion. Because in Quebec, it essentially is.

Jean-Pierre Bouchard saw himself as a freedom fighter. The federation saw him as a common thief. The truth, as always, is probably somewhere in between. Somewhere sticky.

[VOICE:dave] What I find remarkable is that this story has everything a great heist movie needs. A charismatic leader. An inside man. A bumbling crew. A vast fortune. A dramatic discovery — Marcel tapping that empty barrel and hearing it ring like a church bell. A dogged detective who had to keep a straight face on international television. Cousin Raymond, who couldn't keep a secret for eleven minutes. Big Mike reading Sun Tzu in the cab of a truck with his name on it. And at the center of it all... pancake topping. [deadpan] Canada, you never disappoint.

# The Sticky Fingers — Episode 2: The Heist
### A True Crime Podcast — Based on a True Story. Mostly.

*[MUSIC - INTRO]*

## Recap

[HOST] Last time on The Sticky Fingers, we met the Federation of Quebec Maple Syrup Producers — a legal cartel controlling seventy-seven percent of the world's maple syrup supply. We learned about their strategic reserve: thirty million dollars in syrup, stored in a nondescript warehouse in the Quebec countryside, protected by a padlock and a security guard named Steve. And we met the crew: JP Bouchard, a charming ringleader with a Robin Hood complex. Luc Desmarais, the nervous insider with the keys. Cousin Raymond, who cannot keep a secret. And Big Mike Michaud, a trucker who thought he was hauling trees.

[VOICE:dave] He was not hauling trees.

[HOST] No, Danny. He was not. [PAUSE] This is The Sticky Fingers. I'm Marc Thibodeau. And today, we're going to tell you exactly how they did it.

[VOICE:dave] All three thousand tons of it.

## Segment 1: Phase One — The Siphon

[HOST] What happened next was the largest agricultural theft in Canadian history. And it started with a hand pump and a garden hose.

[VOICE:dave] Every great crime begins at the hardware store.

[HOST] It was October 2011. The leaves had turned. The tourists had gone home. The warehouse in Saint-Louis-de-Blandford sat in the flat grey countryside like a forgotten shipping container, which, in many ways, is exactly what it was. Inside: row after row of steel barrels, stacked three high, each one containing six hundred pounds of Grade A maple syrup. The Federation's strategic reserve. Canada's liquid gold. [PAUSE] And it had no alarm system.

[VOICE:dave] None.

[HOST] No security cameras.

[VOICE:dave] Zero.

[HOST] Steve, the security guard, clocked out at five PM sharp every day. He was a man of routine. A man of principle. A man who did not work overtime.

[VOICE:dave] Steve had boundaries. You have to respect that.

[HOST] So the plan was elegant in its simplicity. Luc Desmarais had the keys. He was a licensed dealer, authorized to enter the warehouse during business hours to inspect product. But nobody checked whether he came back after dark. Nobody checked anything. [sighs] Under cover of darkness, JP and Luc would enter the warehouse. They'd select barrels from the back rows — the ones least likely to be inspected. Using hand pumps and lengths of garden hose purchased from a Canadian Tire in Drummondville, they would siphon the syrup out of the steel barrels and into plastic totes. Then they'd refill the original barrels with water, reseal them, and put them back exactly where they found them.

[VOICE:dave] The barrels still weighed six hundred pounds. They just weren't worth eighteen hundred dollars anymore.

[HOST] Each barrel held roughly six hundred pounds of syrup, valued at approximately six hundred and fifty dollars wholesale. They could do four barrels a night. That's twenty-six hundred dollars in syrup, every single night, and nobody was the wiser.

[VOICE:dave] For context, six hundred pounds is the weight of three adult beavers. Give or take.

[HOST] I'm not going to verify that.

[VOICE:dave] You don't have to. It's roughly correct.

[HOST] The syrup needed to move. You can't just stack twenty thousand pounds of stolen maple syrup in your garage. Well, you can. JP did, briefly. But it's not a long-term solution. For that, they needed a trucker. They needed Big Mike.

[PAUSE]

[HOST] We spoke with Big Mike Michaud about his first run.

[VOICE:arnold] JP calls me up. Middle of the week. Says he needs me to drive some product. I ask what kind. He says, and I remember this very clearly, he says, "It's syrup, Mike." [clears throat] And I said, "Like, maple syrup?" And he said, "What other kind of syrup is there, Mike?" Which, fair point.

[HOST] That first shipment was twenty thousand pounds. Plastic totes loaded into a refrigerated trailer. The route: Saint-Louis-de-Blandford to Brampton, Ontario. Five hours through the night.

[VOICE:arnold] You ever driven through northern Ontario at three in the morning with twenty thousand pounds of stolen syrup? It is very quiet. And you have a lot of time to think about your life choices. The highway is empty. The trees are black. Every set of headlights coming toward you, you think, that's it, that's the police. But it never is. It's just another trucker hauling lumber or auto parts or whatever normal people haul.

[HOST] Big Mike made the run without incident. Almost.

[VOICE:arnold] I got pulled over once. In Hawkesbury. Right near the Ontario border. OPP officer. Routine check. He asks what I'm carrying. And I look at him and I say, "Artisanal water. From Quebec. Very exclusive." And he looked at the manifest, and he looked at me, and he said, "Have a good night." And that was that.

[VOICE:dave] To be fair, artisanal water from Quebec is a thing that exists.

[HOST] It absolutely is. You can buy it at Whole Foods.

[VOICE:dave] Twelve dollars a bottle.

[VOICE:arnold] The officer was very polite about it. He shone his flashlight on the totes. They were opaque. He didn't open them. I think he just wanted to make sure I wasn't hauling something dangerous. And I suppose, technically, maple syrup is not dangerous. Unless you're the Federation.

[HOST] Big Mike made fourteen runs that first winter. Always the same route. Always between midnight and dawn. Always alone.

[VOICE:arnold] JP offered to send someone with me once. For company. I said no. When you're doing something like this, you don't want company. You want silence and a clear road and the knowledge that if something goes wrong, it's only on you.

[VOICE:dave] Big Mike is a surprisingly reflective criminal.

[HOST] He is. He really is.

[HOST] The syrup was delivered to a buyer in Brampton who mixed it with legitimately sourced product and sold it across Ontario and into the United States. Some of it went further — down to Vermont and New Hampshire, where it was repackaged under local labels. American consumers were unknowingly eating stolen Canadian syrup on their Sunday morning pancakes. The money came back to JP in cash. Bundles of twenties and fifties, stuffed into hockey bags and driven back to Quebec. And the operation continued.

## Segment 2: Phase Two — The Escalation

[HOST] By spring of 2012, JP Bouchard had a problem. Not a legal problem. Not yet. A math problem. [PAUSE] Four barrels a night, at six hundred and fifty dollars per barrel, was good money. But JP had done the calculations. At that rate, it would take years to make a serious dent in the reserve. And JP was not a patient man.

[VOICE:dave] Subtlety was never JP's strongest quality.

[HOST] So the operation changed. They stopped siphoning. They stopped refilling barrels with water. They stopped being careful. Instead, they started taking whole barrels. Rolling them out of the warehouse on hand trucks, loading them directly onto trailers. Thirty to forty barrels a night. [paper rustling] At peak efficiency, they were moving twenty-four thousand pounds of syrup in a single evening.

[VOICE:dave] That is approximately one hundred and twenty beavers.

[HOST] Nobody noticed. Not Steve, who continued to clock out at five. Not the Federation's annual inspectors, who walked through the warehouse once a year, counted the rows of barrels, and never once opened one. The barrels looked the same from the outside. Full or empty, steel looks like steel.

[VOICE:dave] They had a clipboard. They checked boxes. The boxes were checked.

[HOST] But the crew was not the only vulnerability. There was also Cousin Raymond. Raymond, who had been told about the operation because JP trusted family. Raymond, who could not help himself. [PAUSE] Raymond told his barber.

[VOICE:dave] As one does.

[HOST] The barber told his wife. His wife told her book club.

[VOICE:dave] The book club was reading a heist novel at the time. They thought Raymond was doing a character study.

[HOST] And this is perhaps the most Canadian element of the entire story. A dozen people in a small Quebec town knew that millions of dollars in maple syrup were being stolen from a federal reserve, and not one of them called the police.

[VOICE:dave] The Canadian code of silence. Very real. Very polite.

[HOST] They weren't protecting JP out of loyalty or fear. They just didn't want to cause a fuss. Besides, half the town had opinions about the Federation anyway. The quotas, the price controls, the inspections. A lot of people in maple country felt the Federation had it coming.

[VOICE:dave] The enemy of my enemy is the guy stealing syrup from my enemy.

[HOST] Exactly. There was a certain folk-hero quality to the whole thing. JP wasn't robbing a bank. He was robbing a bureaucracy. And in rural Quebec, that distinction matters.

[PAUSE]

[HOST] There's a subplot here that I want to touch on, because it speaks to the collateral damage of this crime. A man named Henri Delorme, a third-generation maple syrup producer from Lac-Megantic, had entered his masterpiece into the provincial maple syrup competition. He called it Lot 7. A late-harvest amber with what Henri described as "notes of caramel, vanilla, and the first morning of autumn."

[VOICE:dave] There is a provincial maple syrup competition.

[HOST] With certified judges.

[VOICE:dave] Certified.

[HOST] Henri had been working toward this for fifteen years. He'd refined his technique. He'd invested in new equipment. He'd stored Lot 7 in the Federation's reserve for safekeeping, as many producers did. And JP's crew stole it. They didn't know what they had. They didn't care. Lot 7, Henri's life's work, ended up at a chain restaurant in New Brunswick, served alongside bacon and eggs for four dollars a litre.

[VOICE:dave] Henri is still upset about this.

[HOST] His lawyer was not unavailable for comment.

[VOICE:dave] A careful phrasing.

[HOST] Over the course of roughly a year, from late 2011 to mid-2012, JP Bouchard's crew stole more than three thousand tons of maple syrup from the Federation's strategic reserve. The total value: eighteen point seven million dollars.

[VOICE:dave] Enough syrup to cover every pancake in Canada for... probably about a week.

[HOST] The syrup was trafficked in small batches. Mixed with legally sourced product. Distributed across four provinces and into the northeastern United States. The money was laundered through a network of shell companies and sympathetic dealers. It was, by any measure, a sophisticated operation.

[VOICE:dave] Run by a man who bought his equipment at Canadian Tire.

[HOST] The paper trail was thin. The product was nearly untraceable once it entered the legitimate supply chain. Maple syrup doesn't have serial numbers. It doesn't have fingerprints. One barrel of Grade A amber looks exactly like every other barrel of Grade A amber. Or so they thought.

[VOICE:dave] Maple syrup, as it turns out, does have fingerprints. Chemical ones.

[HOST] But we'll get to that. In episode four.

## Segment 3: The Loose Threads

[HOST] So by the summer of 2012, the operation was running smoothly. JP Bouchard was getting rich. Luc Desmarais was getting anxious. Big Mike Michaud was getting philosophical. And Raymond was still talking.

[VOICE:dave] Raymond never stopped talking.

[HOST] We spoke with Luc about this period.

[VOICE:adam] Every morning I'd drive past the warehouse and think, is today the day? [sighs] Every time the phone rang, I assumed it was the police. It was usually JP asking me to open the warehouse again. I stopped sleeping. I stopped eating. My wife thought I was having an affair. I told her I was. It was easier to explain than the truth.

[HOST] Luc had developed what his doctor later described as stress-related gastric distress. He was losing weight. His hands shook. He jumped at doorbells.

[VOICE:dave] The man was falling apart, and nobody noticed because they were all too busy stealing syrup.

[HOST] Meanwhile, Big Mike had started keeping a journal. He told us he wanted to process his feelings.

[VOICE:dave] About the syrup.

[HOST] About the syrup. About the drives. About what he called "the moral ambiguity of the condiment supply chain." He showed us an excerpt. It reads, and I'm quoting directly: "Kilometre 347. The moon is full over Algonquin Park. I am carrying twelve tons of another man's sweetness. Who am I? What have I become?"

[VOICE:dave] Big Mike contains multitudes.

[HOST] He does. He genuinely does. [PAUSE] We also spoke with JP.

[VOICE:josh] Looking back, the signs were there. Raymond was telling everyone. Big Mike was writing his memoirs. Luc was developing an ulcer you could see from space. But when you're making that kind of money... you convince yourself it'll last forever. [PAUSE] You tell yourself, one more run. One more month. One more trailer. And then you'll stop. But you never stop. Because the warehouse is right there, and the barrels are right there, and nobody is watching.

[HOST] It would not last forever.

[VOICE:dave] Nothing does. Especially not three thousand tons of misplaced maple syrup.

[HOST] Nobody was watching. But the barrels were getting lighter. The water-filled ones from the early days sloshed when you moved them. The empty ones from the later phase were featherlight. Three thousand tons of absence, hiding in plain sight.

[VOICE:dave] Three thousand tons is a lot of absence.

[HOST] It was. And it was about to be discovered.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] By accident.

[HOST] By complete and total accident. The kind of accident that only happens because someone finally decided to do their job properly. One person, on one afternoon, who didn't just count the barrels. Who actually touched one.

[VOICE:dave] Imagine being the guy who discovers an eighteen-million-dollar crime because you knocked on a barrel.

[HOST] We don't have to imagine. We talked to him. But that's next week.

## Closing

[HOST] Three thousand tons gone. The reserve gutted. Eighteen point seven million dollars in maple syrup, vanished into the North American breakfast supply chain. And nobody knew. Not the Federation. Not the inspectors. Not Steve. [PAUSE] But in July of 2012, a man named Marcel Gagnon — a mid-level Federation employee conducting a routine inventory — would climb a stack of barrels in the far corner of the warehouse. He would tap the top barrel with his knuckle, the way you'd tap a watermelon at the grocery store. And the barrel would ring. Not the dull thud of six hundred pounds of liquid. A ring. Hollow. Empty. Like a church bell. [PAUSE] Next time on The Sticky Fingers: The Barrel That Rang.

[VOICE:dave] It rang like a church bell. And it changed everything.

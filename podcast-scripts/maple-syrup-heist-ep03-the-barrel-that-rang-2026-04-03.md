# The Sticky Fingers — Episode 3: The Barrel That Rang
### A True Crime Podcast — Based on a True Story. Mostly.

*[MUSIC - INTRO]*

## Recap

[HOST] Previously on The Sticky Fingers. A crew of four men, led by a charming ringleader named Jean-Pierre Bouchard, spent the better part of a year siphoning maple syrup from the Federation of Quebec Maple Syrup Producers' strategic reserve. Three thousand tons of it. Eighteen point seven million dollars. They replaced it with water. They stole whole barrels. They drove trucks in and out of a warehouse guarded by one man named Steve, a padlock, and absolutely no cameras. Nobody noticed. Nobody checked. Nobody asked why the reserve smelled faintly of larceny.

[PAUSE]

[HOST] But in July 2012, a federation inspector named Marcel Gagnon climbed a stack of barrels. And everything changed.

## Segment 1: The Discovery

[HOST] July 2012. Quebec was coming off a record maple season. Prices were strong, demand was high, and the federation's strategic reserve was supposedly sitting at full capacity across six warehouses in the town of Saint-Louis-de-Blandford. Population: roughly twelve hundred. Crime rate: historically, zero.

[HOST] Every summer, the federation conducts a routine inventory audit. Someone has to go into each warehouse, count the barrels, verify the lot numbers, check the seals. It is, by all accounts, the least exciting job in Canadian agriculture. And for eleven consecutive years, that job belonged to Marcel Gagnon.

[HOST] I visited Marcel at his home in Drummondville. He offered me tea before I had taken off my coat. He was wearing a cardigan. He has a collection of model trains in his basement. I mention this because it tells you everything you need to know about the kind of man Marcel Gagnon is. Thorough. Patient. A man who counts things for a living and finds genuine satisfaction in it.

[VOICE:brian] I had been inspecting the reserve for eleven years. Eleven years of counting barrels. It is not glamorous work. But someone has to do it.

[HOST] Marcel told me he had never found a single discrepancy in eleven years. Not one. Every barrel accounted for, every lot number matching the manifest. He said this with pride. He said it the way a man talks about a perfect attendance record at church.

[HOST] July twentieth. Warehouse number four. Marcel arrived at eight in the morning with his clipboard, his reading glasses, and a thermos of coffee. The warehouse held approximately fourteen thousand barrels, stacked three high on wooden pallets, stretching in rows from one end of the building to the other. The air smelled like maple. It always smelled like maple. He began at row one and worked his way methodically toward the back.

[VOICE:brian] I needed to verify a lot number near the top of a stack in the northeast corner. So I climbed up. I have done this hundreds of times. You put your foot on the lower barrel, you grab the rim of the upper one, and you pull yourself up. Routine.

[HOST] Except this time, when Marcel put his hand on the barrel to steady himself, something happened.

[VOICE:brian] It shifted.

[PAUSE]

[VOICE:brian] A full barrel does not shift. A full barrel weighs six hundred pounds. You could lean your entire body weight against it and it would not move. This one slid like it was on ice.

[HOST] The barrel tipped off the stack. Marcel lost his grip and fell backward, landing on the concrete floor. The barrel came down a fraction of a second later.

[VOICE:brian] If I had been standing six inches to the left, I would not be here talking to you. Six hundred pounds of steel falling from eight feet. But it was not six hundred pounds. That was the thing. I watched a barrel that should have been full of maple syrup bounce off the concrete floor like a basketball.

[gasps]

[HOST] Marcel lay on the floor of warehouse four, staring at a forty-five-gallon steel drum that had just bounced. He got to his feet. He walked over to the barrel. He knocked on the side.

[VOICE:brian] It rang like a church bell. Hollow. Completely hollow.

[HOST] He opened it. Empty. He opened the next one. Water. The one after that. Water. The one after that. Empty.

[VOICE:brian] I opened forty barrels that afternoon. Thirty-seven were either empty or full of water.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] That's a ninety-two point five percent fraud rate, for those keeping score at home.

[HOST] Marcel sat down on the warehouse floor. He stared at the ceiling. He set his clipboard on his lap.

[VOICE:brian] I sat there for a very long time. And I thought: either this is the worst clerical error in Canadian history, or something very, very bad has happened.

[HOST] Marcel did what Marcel does. He filed a report. He went back to his office, sat down at his computer, and typed a memo to federation headquarters. Subject line: "Critical Inventory Discrepancy — Immediate Attention Required."

[HOST] He used red font. He triple-underlined the word "critical." He flagged it as urgent priority. He attached photographs. He sent it to four people. He cc'd two more, just to be safe.

[VOICE:brian] I went home that night and I could not sleep. I kept thinking about the sound. That hollow ringing. I must have opened and closed my refrigerator six times, just listening to things that sounded solid. My wife asked me if I was feeling all right.

[PAUSE]

[HOST] Nobody read the report for two weeks.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] In fairness, it was filed behind a stack of parking validations and a formal complaint about the vending machine in Building C.

[HOST] The vending machine.

[VOICE:dave] Building C's vending machine had been stealing people's loonies for months. Truly, a crime wave.

[HOST] When federation president Colette Fontaine finally learned the scope of what had happened, she was at an agriculture conference in Montreal. I spoke with her by phone.

[VOICE:rachel] The lieutenant called me on my cell phone during a panel on sustainable tapping practices. He said, "Colette, we have a problem." I assumed it was the vending machine again.

[HOST] It was not the vending machine.

[VOICE:rachel] He said, "Most of it. Most of the reserve." And I said, "Most of what?" And he said, "Most of the syrup. It's gone." I had to sit down.

[PAUSE]

[VOICE:rachel] Eighteen point seven million dollars. Gone. Replaced with water.

[HOST] What did you do?

[VOICE:rachel] I believe my exact words were... unprintable. And then I called the police. And then I called my therapist.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] The federation's insurance did not cover "replacement of syrup with water." They checked.

[HOST] Colette told me she spent the next seventy-two hours in emergency meetings. The board convened. Lawyers were called. Accountants were woken up at home. Someone suggested checking whether the water in the barrels was at least clean. It was not.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] Insult to injury.

[HOST] The police were formally contacted on August fifth, 2012. The largest heist in Canadian history was now a police matter. And somewhere in a barbershop in Trois-Rivieres, cousin Raymond was still talking.

## Segment 2: Operation Maple

[HOST] The Surete du Quebec assembled a task force. They called it Operation Maple. Because of course they did.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] Bold name. Very covert.

[HOST] The task force was led by Sergeant Diane Pelletier, a twelve-year veteran of the provincial police who had just transferred from the homicide division in Quebec City. She had a reputation for being meticulous, relentless, and humourless in exactly the right proportion. I should note that she did not request this transfer.

[VOICE:sarah] When my captain told me I was being assigned to a syrup theft, I thought it was hazing. I had just closed a double homicide in Sherbrooke. I had commendations. And now I was being told to investigate missing pancake ingredients.

[HOST] Her colleagues were supportive, in their way.

[VOICE:sarah] They left Aunt Jemima bottles on my desk. One of them wrapped my service weapon in a pancake. A real pancake. I had to file a report about it.

[HOST] But the case was no joke. Eighteen point seven million dollars. Potentially the largest agricultural theft in North American history. And Pelletier quickly discovered that investigating it presented unique challenges.

[VOICE:sarah] Syrup doesn't have serial numbers. You can't dust it for fingerprints. Once it's mixed with other syrup, it's gone. It dissolves into the supply chain. We were looking for a liquid needle in a liquid haystack.

[HOST] She assembled a team of eight investigators, two translators — because the paperwork was in both French and English and the legal terminology did not always translate cleanly — and a forensic accountant from Montreal.

[VOICE:sarah] On his first day, he told me this was "the stickiest case he'd ever worked." I reassigned him.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] Harsh but fair.

[HOST] The team began following the money. Shipping records. Trucking manifests. Export declarations. Somewhere, three thousand tons of maple syrup had been sold, and the proceeds had gone into someone's pocket. Syrup that heavy does not move quietly. It requires trucks, warehouses, buyers, distributors. It leaves a paper trail. You just have to know where to look.

[HOST] And then they caught their break.

[PAUSE]

[HOST] Cousin Raymond.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] Of course.

[HOST] Raymond Bouchard, the crew's logistics coordinator and the man who, as we established in episode two, could not stop telling people about the heist. He had been bragging at a bar in Trois-Rivieres. Not quietly. Not in a corner. At the bar. To anyone who would listen. He told the bartender — a woman named Suzanne — over his fourth beer of the evening, that he was, and I am quoting directly here, "a major player in the syrup underworld."

[VOICE:sarah] The bartender mentioned it to her brother-in-law at a family barbecue. Her brother-in-law happened to be a retired RCMP officer.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] That is the most Canadian chain of custody I have ever heard.

[HOST] The retired officer called a friend at the Surete. The friend called Pelletier. Pelletier brought Raymond in for questioning on a Tuesday afternoon in October.

[VOICE:sarah] He lasted eleven minutes.

[HOST] Eleven minutes.

[VOICE:sarah] Eleven minutes. He gave us everything. Names, dates, locations, buyers, prices, routes. He drew us a map on a napkin. He asked if he could get a reduced sentence for cooperating. We had not yet charged him with anything.

[HOST] With Raymond's information, the investigation expanded rapidly. The syrup had moved through a network that stretched across three provinces and into the United States. Pelletier traced shipments to Ontario, to New Brunswick, and across the border to Vermont.

[HOST] In New Brunswick, they found something remarkable. An elderly businessman named Etienne Lafleur, operating out of a converted barn on a quiet rural property, running what can only be described as a syrup laundering operation.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] Syrup laundering.

[HOST] Syrup laundering. Lafleur would purchase stolen syrup at a fraction of the market rate, re-barrel it, forge new federation seals, and sell it as legitimate product to distributors in the Maritimes and New England. He had been doing this for years. He had a filing system. He kept receipts. He had a ledger, handwritten, in a leather-bound notebook. He was seventy-three years old, a grandfather of six, and showed no signs of remorse. When officers arrived at his property, he offered them coffee and asked if they wanted to see his operation. He seemed proud of it.

[HOST] Meanwhile, Pelletier's undercover officers were infiltrating what she described, with visible discomfort, as the maple syrup black market.

[VOICE:sarah] It operates out of pancake houses. Diners. Sugar shacks. We had officers sitting in IHOP parking lots for three weeks, watching transactions. One of them gained nine pounds. He filed for hazard pay. It was denied.

[HOST] Over the course of the investigation, Pelletier became, somewhat against her will, one of the province's foremost experts on maple syrup.

[VOICE:sarah] I can tell you the difference between Grade A Golden and Grade A Dark by smell alone. I have a certificate in syrup grading from the International Maple Syrup Institute. It is framed next to my commendation for bravery. They are the same size.

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] Priorities.

[HOST] By early 2013, Pelletier had mapped the entire operation. She knew who stole the syrup, who moved it, who sold it, and where the money went. She had twenty-two suspects across three provinces. She was ready to move. But coordinating arrests across Quebec, Ontario, and New Brunswick required planning. And then there was the matter of the international border.

[VOICE:sarah] Some of the syrup had been sold in Vermont. Which meant we needed cooperation from American law enforcement. I called the FBI field office in Burlington, Vermont.

[PAUSE]

[VOICE:sarah] There was a very long silence on the other end of the phone.

[HOST] What did they say?

[VOICE:sarah] The agent asked me to repeat myself. And then he asked me if this was a prank. And then he put me on hold for nine minutes. When he came back, he said, and I quote, "Ma'am, we will... look into it."

[VOICE:dave] [deadpan] The full resources of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, brought to bear on maple syrup.

[HOST] To their credit, the FBI did cooperate. Eventually. After Pelletier sent them a forty-page briefing document with colour-coded charts and a glossary of maple syrup terminology.

[VOICE:sarah] I included a glossary because the first agent I spoke with did not know what a sugar bush was. I did not want that to be an obstacle.

[clears throat]

[HOST] Pelletier told me there was a moment, late one night in her office, surrounded by shipping manifests and syrup grading charts, when she stepped back and looked at the evidence board. Photographs, red string, pushpins. A map of eastern North America with arrows connecting sugar shacks to warehouses to diners to bank accounts.

[VOICE:sarah] It looked exactly like a narcotics investigation. Exactly. Except instead of cocaine, every single arrow pointed to breakfast.

[HOST] The pieces were in place. The warrants were drafted. The task force was coordinated across four jurisdictions. For maple syrup.

## SEGMENT 1 — The Town By the Numbers

[HOST] [paper rustling] First, let's set the scene. Lunenburg is *small.* Population: roughly **2,400 people.** That's it. About the size of a mid-size apartment building in Toronto. On a busy summer day, the number of tourists walking the streets can easily outnumber the residents.

[HOST] The town skews older — the average age here is **52.2 years,** and more than a third of residents are seniors. That's not surprising for a place where the economy has historically been shaped by industries — fishing, shipbuilding — that have shed jobs over the decades. Young people leave to find work, and retirees arrive to find beauty.

[HOST] [sighs] The median household income sits at around **$63,200 a year** — not poverty by any stretch, but not wealthy either, especially given what has happened to property values.

[HOST] [gasps] Because here is the jaw-dropping statistic: between 2019 and 2022 — just three years — the median home price in Lunenburg increased by **105 percent.** One hundred and five percent. During the same period it had risen only 22% in the preceding three years.

[HOST] The pandemic-era migration surge, when remote workers fled cities for scenic small towns with fast internet, hit places like Lunenburg *hard.* Today, an estimated **99 percent** of single-person households in town earn below the income required to afford the median home sale price. Ninety-nine percent.

[HOST] [playfully] So if you want to live alone in Lunenburg and own your home — statistically, good luck. The colourful houses look great on Instagram. Affording them is another story.

*[PAUSE]*

## SEGMENT 2 — The UNESCO Burden: $46 Million and Counting

[HOST] [clears throat] Now let's talk about the elephant in the harbour — or rather, the **$46 million** elephant.

[HOST] Being a UNESCO World Heritage Site is, on the surface, a tremendous honour. The designation attracts tourists, raises the town's global profile, and commits the community to maintaining its architectural character. But it comes with costs that a town of 2,400 people was never built to absorb on its own.

[HOST] [curious] The town has identified **ten major heritage assets** — public buildings, historic structures, key infrastructure — that require maintenance and repair totalling an estimated $46 million. For context: Lunenburg's entire annual municipal budget is a fraction of that. This isn't a theoretical problem. Buildings are aging. Maintenance has been deferred. The bill is coming due.

[HOST] Heritage preservation in Lunenburg means strict rules about what you can and can't do to your property. Colours, materials, window styles, roof lines — all regulated. For homeowners, this can mean that fixing a rotting window frame costs twice what it might anywhere else, because you're required to use heritage-appropriate materials and methods.

[HOST] [sarcastic] Nothing says "charming small town" like a heritage compliance form.

[HOST] And the tensions aren't just financial. In 2024, the town council was considering developing a nine-hectare parcel of land called **Blockhouse Hill** — building about 250 new housing units to address the affordability crisis. Sounds reasonable, right? The problem: any significant new development on or near the historic hillside risked triggering a review by UNESCO that could potentially affect the town's World Heritage designation.

[HOST] Council ultimately reversed course and halted the development plan. Housing need on one hand. UNESCO status on the other. There's no easy answer when your most valuable asset is also your biggest constraint.

## SEGMENT 3 — A Town of Artists and Festivals

[HOST] [cheerfully] Okay. Enough hard news. Let's talk about what makes Lunenburg *genuinely wonderful* to visit and — for those who can afford it — to live in.

[HOST] The arts scene here is astonishing for a town this size. About **10 percent of local businesses** are in some way connected to the arts — galleries, pottery studios, craft shops, design studios. There are at least **eight art galleries** within the Old Town. The streets attract creative people the way lighthouses attract ships, and that critical mass of artists and makers gives Lunenburg a cultural vitality that punches far above its demographic weight.

[HOST] [excited] Then there are the festivals. The **Lunenburg Folk Harbour Festival** — Nova Scotia's longest-running folk music festival — takes over the town every August, filling the waterfront with traditional and contemporary folk from across Canada and beyond. Picture a warm Maritime evening, a glass of local wine, and a fiddle echoing off 250-year-old buildings. It's genuinely magical.

[HOST] There's also the **Festival of Crafts,** the **Street Art Festival,** and the **Folk Art Festival** — which brings together the distinctly Nova Scotian tradition of folk art: brightly painted wooden sculptures, whirligigs, hand-carved fish, and the kind of work that is deeply rooted in the same German and Acadian traditions that shaped this community.

[HOST] [calm] And of course there is the **Fisheries Museum of the Atlantic** — one of the finest maritime museums in Canada — sitting right on the waterfront. The museum tells the story of the Grand Banks fishery, of the dorymen who worked in brutal conditions for generations, and of the Bluenose herself. Bluenose II — the replica schooner — docks here when she's not sailing, and you can tour her decks.

[HOST] [paper rustling] The waterfront is still a *working* harbour. This is important to understand. Lunenburg didn't hollow itself out and become a theme park. Fishing boats still come in and unload their catch. The smells are real. The industry is real — if reduced from its peak. This is a living town, not a preserved specimen.

## SEGMENT 4 — The Tourism Question

[HOST] [sighs] But tourism — and the tensions it creates — are impossible to ignore.

[HOST] Lunenburg draws visitors from around the world. On a peak summer weekend, the streets of a town of 2,400 can feel *crowded.* The harbour fills with sailboats and whale-watching vessels. The restaurants and galleries do brisk business. The local economy depends on those tourist dollars.

[HOST] [curious] But the mayor has had to publicly acknowledge that Lunenburg has a serious **RV problem.** Large motorhomes — some of them enormous — cram onto the narrow historic streets that were designed for horses and foot traffic in the 18th century. Overnight "boondocking" — parking an RV and sleeping in it without paying for a campground — has become such a problem that the town has struggled to find solutions.

[HOST] [laughs] Imagine planning a romantic getaway to a UNESCO World Heritage Site and waking up next to a 40-foot diesel motorhome with a satellite dish.

[HOST] There's also a deeper, more philosophical tension in the tourism boom: the risk that as Lunenburg becomes more famous, it becomes less *itself.* That the authentic fishing community gets slowly replaced by boutique shops and vacation rentals. That the people who actually built and sustained this culture get priced out of it.

[HOST] [calm] It's not a uniquely Lunenburg problem — it's happening in beautiful historic towns everywhere. But it feels more acute here, because so much of what makes Lunenburg worth preserving is not just the buildings — it's the *people* and the *community* they sustain.

## SEGMENT 5 — Life in the Slow Lane

[HOST] [calm] Despite the pressures, there is something about Lunenburg that continues to draw people — and to hold them.

[HOST] Residents describe a quality of life that's genuinely hard to replicate. In a town of 2,400, you know your neighbours. You know your butcher, your pharmacist, your local councillor. Community is not an abstraction — it is Tuesday afternoon at the farmers' market, or Saturday morning at the harbour watching the fishing boats.

[HOST] [curious] The town has won the **Communities in Bloom** award for most beautiful small town in Canada. It's the kind of designation that gets mocked until you stand on the waterfront and look up at the hill — the Crimson and ochre and sage-green houses rising above the masts, the church steeples poking above the roofline, the water reflecting it all back — and then you understand completely.

[HOST] There is also something quietly remarkable about the **continuity** here. Lunenburg families have been fishing, building, and running businesses from the same streets for six, seven, eight generations. Last names you see on storefronts today are names that appear in the archives from the 1760s. That kind of deep-rooted community identity is vanishingly rare in modern North America.

[HOST] [cheerfully] And the food — I'd be remiss not to mention the food. Lunenburg sausage — a spiced smoked pork sausage that is a direct descendant of German butchering traditions brought over in 1753 — is a regional institution. You can still buy it from local butchers. It is delicious and it is *history on a plate.*

[HOST] [playfully] Don't sleep on the fish chowder either.

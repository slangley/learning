[HOST] [calm] There is a park at the north end of Halifax, on a gentle slope that runs down to the Bedford Basin. It has a community centre, a beach, a replica of a small church. On a clear day you can see across the water to the hills of Dartmouth. It's a peaceful spot. A lot of people in Halifax walk their dogs there.

[HOST] Not many of them know what used to stand on that land.

[HOST] Africville was a community. It had been there since the early 1800s — a tight-knit Black neighbourhood that had survived and grown for more than a century, built by the descendants of Black Loyalists, freed slaves, and men and women who had come to Nova Scotia looking for something the rest of the city was mostly unwilling to give them. By the 1950s, Africville had about 400 residents. It had a church — the Seaview United Baptist Church, founded in 1849 — that was the spiritual and community heart of the neighbourhood. It had homes passed down through generations. It had families who had been there for six generations.

[HOST] In the 1960s, the City of Halifax demolished it. They used garbage trucks to move people's belongings.

[HOST] This is Episode 5 of *The Warden of the North*. This one is about the parts of Halifax's history that didn't make the tourist brochures — the scars that the city spent decades trying not to look at, and the slow, incomplete work of looking at them honestly.

## SEGMENT 1 — The Long Neglect

[HOST] [paper rustling] To understand Africville, you have to understand what was done to it before it was destroyed. Because the destruction wasn't a sudden decision. It was the endpoint of a hundred years of deliberate neglect.

[HOST] The Black Loyalists who settled in Nova Scotia after the American Revolution were, from the beginning, given the worst of everything. The worst land. The fewest legal protections. The least access to the educational and economic institutions that the white Loyalists were building around them. Some of the communities founded in these early years — Birchtown, Preston, Hammonds Plains — were among the poorest places in the province for generations. Africville, at the north end of Halifax peninsula, was another such community.

[HOST] [sighs] What the City of Halifax did to Africville over the 19th and 20th centuries is almost methodical in its cruelty. The residents paid property taxes. They did not receive services. No paved roads. No running water. No sewers — not until 1957, and even then only partially. While the rest of Halifax was being connected to the modern urban infrastructure of the 20th century, Africville was deliberately left out.

[HOST] And worse than the neglect: the city kept placing its most unwanted facilities next door to Africville. A slaughterhouse. A fertilizer plant. Rockhead Prison, built in 1854. The city's infectious diseases hospital in the 1870s. A dump — an open, burning city dump — placed directly adjacent to the neighbourhood in 1955. The people of Africville could smell the dump from their homes. They complained. Nothing was done.

[HOST] [curious] Every one of these decisions required a city council vote. Every one of them was a choice. The argument that gets made — that the city simply didn't think about Africville, that it was oversight rather than intention — becomes harder and harder to sustain when you look at the pattern. The city saw Africville. It just didn't see the people of Africville as deserving the same consideration it gave everyone else.

[HOST] By the early 1960s, the city had decided that Africville was a slum, and that the humane thing to do was to relocate its residents to better housing elsewhere in the city. The language used was the language of social welfare. The implementation was something else entirely.

## SEGMENT 2 — Africville: The Demolition

[HOST] [calm] The formal relocation of Africville took place between 1964 and 1967. The city's public position was that this was urban renewal — that it was helping the residents of Africville by moving them to better housing. But the way it was done tells a different story.

[HOST] Residents who could prove they owned their land — who had deeds — were offered compensation equal to the assessed value of their homes. Many residents could not produce deeds. Their families had been there for generations, had paid taxes, had lived their lives on that land, but in a city that had systematically denied them the same legal formalities it offered white residents, the paper trail was incomplete. Those residents were offered five hundred dollars. Five hundred dollars to leave the home their family had occupied for four or five generations.

[HOST] People's belongings were moved by Halifax city garbage trucks. It is difficult to read that fact without feeling the weight of what it communicated — about how the city regarded these residents, about what the demolition actually was.

[HOST] [sighs] The Seaview United Baptist Church — the community's heart, its gathering place, its spiritual centre for over a century — was demolished. The homes came down. The land was cleared. And for years it sat empty, used eventually as a holding area for bridge construction, before being turned into the park it is now.

[HOST] The displaced residents of Africville were scattered across the city into public housing and rented rooms. The community — the specific, irreplaceable thing that had existed on that land for 160 years — was gone. Studies done in the years afterward found that the quality of life of former Africville residents did not improve after relocation. Many were worse off, by measurable economic metrics, than they had been before.

[HOST] [curious] The fight for recognition and justice began almost immediately. The Africville Action Committee was formed in 1969. The Africville Genealogy Society, founded in the 1980s, documented the community's history and pushed for acknowledgment. The fight took four decades.

[HOST] In 2010, Halifax Mayor Peter Kelly stood in front of former residents of Africville and apologized. The city agreed to a settlement. The replica church was built at the site. The park was named Africville Park.

[HOST] Former residents said the apology was meaningful. They also said it was insufficient. Both of those things can be true at the same time.

## SEGMENT 3 — Post-War Halifax: Building a University City

[HOST] [paper rustling] While Africville's story unfolded through the 1950s and 1960s, the rest of Halifax was transforming in ways that would define what it became.

[HOST] The post-war years brought a question that many mid-sized cities faced: what do you do when you've been a military port for two hundred years and the world doesn't need as many military ports anymore? Halifax had the harbour, the Citadel, the naval base. But it needed an economy that wasn't entirely dependent on defence spending.

[HOST] [calm] The answer, in Halifax's case, turned out to be education. Dalhousie University, founded in 1818, expanded dramatically in the post-war decades. Saint Mary's University grew. The Nova Scotia College of Art and Design — now NSCAD, founded originally in 1887 — became one of the most respected art schools in North America. The Technical University of Nova Scotia merged with Dalhousie in the 1990s, creating one of the larger engineering programs in eastern Canada. Eventually, Halifax would have six universities in a city of a few hundred thousand people — a higher density of post-secondary education per capita than almost anywhere in the country.

[HOST] [curious] That concentration of universities changed Halifax's character. It made it younger than it might otherwise have been. It made it more culturally open. It brought students from across Canada and increasingly from around the world. It built a local publishing scene, a music scene, a visual arts scene. The Halifax sound — whatever that meant at any given decade — was partly a product of having that many young, creative people in a relatively small, walkable city.

[HOST] The music scene is worth pausing on. Halifax in the late 1980s and 1990s became one of the most fertile music scenes in Canada — bands like Sloan, Eric's Trip, Thrush Hermit, the Super Friendz, and later Joel Plaskett and many others built something that got called the Halifax Pop Explosion. Much of it centred around the Halifax Pop Explosion festival, still running today, and a cluster of venues in the north and south ends of the peninsula. It was not a commercial juggernaut. It was a genuine, local, low-budget thing — which is part of what made it lasting.

## SEGMENT 4 — The Province Beyond the City

[HOST] [calm] Halifax is the story of this series, but Nova Scotia is the context. And it's worth spending a moment on what the province looked like in the second half of the 20th century, because the two stories are not the same.

[HOST] Outside Halifax, Nova Scotia was struggling in ways that the growing university city sometimes obscured. The steel mills of Sydney, Cape Breton — once one of the most productive steel operations in North America — began their long decline in the 1960s and were finally closed in 2001. The coal mines of Cape Breton, which had been feeding those mills and fuelling Atlantic Canada for a century, went with them. The fishing industry, which had sustained coastal communities for four hundred years, was devastated by the collapse of the cod stocks in the early 1990s. The moratorium on cod fishing declared in 1992 hit small Nova Scotian towns like a second expulsion.

[HOST] [sighs] Rural Nova Scotia in the late 20th century watched its young people leave. It watched services consolidate in Halifax and disappear from smaller towns. It watched a province that had once had prosperous, self-sufficient communities in dozens of places become increasingly dependent on a single urban centre. The demographic pressure on rural Nova Scotia is real, ongoing, and not easily solved. Small towns along the South Shore, in the Valley, in Cape Breton — they are beautiful places. They are also places where the school sometimes closes because there aren't enough children.

[HOST] [curious] This tension — between a growing, increasingly cosmopolitan Halifax and a struggling rural Nova Scotia — is one of the defining features of the province's politics today. We'll come back to it in the final episode.

[HOST] For now, let me say this about the Halifax that emerged from the second half of the 20th century. It was a city that had looked honestly, if slowly and incompletely, at some of its darkest chapters. The Africville apology happened. The Cornwallis statue came down. The conversations about Mi'kmaq title and treaty rights became more serious and more legally substantive. None of this undoes what was done. But the willingness to look is not nothing.

[HOST] Halifax in 2000 was a more honest city than Halifax in 1950. That's something.

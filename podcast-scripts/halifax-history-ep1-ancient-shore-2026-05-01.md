## SEGMENT 1 — Mi'kma'ki: The World Before the Ships

[HOST] [paper rustling] Ten thousand years. Let that sit for a moment. While the civilizations we learned about in school — Egypt, Greece, Rome — were rising and falling and rising again, people were living on this peninsula, fishing these waters, reading these tides.

[HOST] They called their homeland *Mi'kma'ki*. And they called themselves *Mi'kmaq* — the people. Their territory stretched across what we now know as Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island, parts of Newfoundland, and the Gaspé Peninsula in Québec. By almost any measure, this was one of the most resource-rich coastal environments on the planet. The Bay of Fundy, which forms the western edge of what is now Nova Scotia, has the highest tides on Earth — in some places, the water rises and falls more than sixteen metres twice a day. To live here, you didn't fight that kind of power. You learned to work with it.

[HOST] The Mi'kmaq organized their world into seven districts, each governed by a district chief, and all seven chiefs together formed the Mi'kmaq Grand Council — a governing body that operated by consensus. Not by decree, not by the word of a single king, but by agreement. Historians of governance sometimes point to this as one of the most sophisticated political structures in pre-contact North America.

[HOST] [calm] Their spiritual world was equally complex. The Mi'kmaq did not draw a firm line between the natural and the supernatural. Everything — the sun, the rivers, the rocks, the animals — had spirit. The universe was animated. The figure of *Glooscap*, or *Klu'skap*, was central — a great being who taught the people how the world had come into being, and how to live within it well. Their knowledge of medicinal plants, of seasonal migration patterns, of where the salmon ran and where the moose bedded down in winter — this was the accumulated wisdom of hundreds of generations.

[HOST] And then, one day, the ships came.

[HOST] [sighs] The exact moment is hard to pin down. There were almost certainly Norse contact points centuries before. Giovanni Caboto — the explorer we call John Cabot — almost certainly encountered Mi'kmaq people when he sailed these waters in 1497 on behalf of the English Crown. But sustained contact with Europeans arrived with the French in the early 1600s. And for the Mi'kmaq, the French were a revelation — because the French, at least at first, were not interested in taking their land.

[HOST] The French wanted fur. They wanted fish. They wanted trade partners. And the Mi'kmaq were exceptional trade partners. A commercial relationship grew — and with it, something the Mi'kmaq had never encountered before: genuine cultural exchange. French settlers learned from the Mi'kmaq how to survive these winters, how to navigate these rivers, how to read the landscape. And the Mi'kmaq adopted certain French goods, French tools, even elements of French religion. Intermarriage happened. Children were born who moved comfortably between both worlds.

[HOST] It was imperfect, as all human contact is imperfect. But by the standards of European colonialism in the 1600s, the relationship between the Mi'kmaq and the French settlers of Acadia was remarkably close to something resembling mutual respect.

[HOST] [curious] Which makes what came next all the more catastrophic.

## SEGMENT 2 — Acadia: The Neutral Colony

[HOST] [paper rustling] Port-Royal. 1605. Samuel de Champlain and Pierre du Gua de Monts establish a permanent French settlement on the shores of the Annapolis Basin — on the western side of what is now Nova Scotia. It is the first permanent European settlement north of Florida in North America. Let that land in your brain for a second. Not Plymouth Rock. Not Jamestown. *Port-Royal, Acadia.* Before all of them.

[HOST] Over the next century and a half, something extraordinary grew up along the Bay of Fundy. The Acadians — as these French settlers came to call themselves — built a society that looked almost nothing like France and almost nothing like the other European colonies that were springing up along the Atlantic seaboard.

[HOST] [calm] Here is the detail I always find remarkable. The land around the Bay of Fundy is largely salt marsh — tidal flatland that gets inundated twice a day by those enormous Fundy tides. Most colonists would look at that landscape and see a problem. The Acadians looked at it and saw the richest farmland in the hemisphere, if you could figure out how to drain it.

[HOST] And they did. They built an engineering system called *aboiteaux* — wooden sluices fitted with swinging one-way gates, dug into the base of earthen dykes. Fresh water drained out through the gates at low tide. Salt water couldn't push back in at high tide. Over two to three years, the salt leached out of the reclaimed land, and what you were left with was some of the most fertile soil in North America. They grew wheat, oats, barley, rye, corn, flax. They kept cattle. Their pigs roamed freely through the forest and practically fed themselves.

[HOST] An English officer visiting Acadia in the early 1700s described the Acadian farms as the most productive he had ever seen on the continent — and he was not saying so admiringly. He was saying so with alarm. Because by then, the English wanted this land.

[HOST] [curious] The Acadians had a political stance that was, frankly, ahead of its time and ultimately fatal to them. They called themselves *les neutres* — the neutral ones. When war came between France and England — and war came again and again in the 1600s and 1700s — the Acadians refused to take sides. They would not fight for France against England. They would not swear unconditional loyalty oaths to the British Crown. They spoke French. They were Catholic. They traded with the Mi'kmaq, who they considered neighbors and kin. And they simply wanted to be left alone to farm their marshlands and raise their enormous families.

[HOST] There are records of Acadian women bearing children into their forties. Extended family groups formed small hamlets — two or three houses, sometimes more — where everyone was related, where the building of a dyke or a barn was a community celebration, where the wealthier families shared their surplus with those who had less.

[HOST] It was a society built on kinship, hard work, and a kind of stubborn pragmatic resilience. And in 1710, when the British finally won permanent control of Acadia and renamed it Nova Scotia — *New Scotland* — that resilience was about to be tested to destruction.

## SEGMENT 3 — The Tightening Vice

[HOST] [clears throat] For more than four decades after 1710, the British tried to figure out what to do with the Acadians. There were roughly fourteen thousand of them by the 1750s — a substantial, rooted population that spoke the wrong language, practiced the wrong religion, and refused to sign the right paperwork.

[HOST] The British kept asking the Acadians to swear an unconditional oath of allegiance to the Crown — meaning they would fight for Britain if called upon. The Acadians kept saying no. They would swear a limited oath — yes, we'll be peaceful, we won't take up arms against you — but unconditional? No. Not that.

[HOST] [sighs] And then there's the Mi'kmaq situation, which the British were watching with mounting anxiety. Because the Mi'kmaq had not stopped resisting British expansion. They were conducting raids on British settlements. They were allied, spiritually and practically, with the Acadians. In British eyes, the Acadians and the Mi'kmaq together represented a permanent, French-speaking, Catholic, potentially hostile population sitting in the middle of their most strategically important North American territory.

[HOST] In 1749 — the very year the British founded Halifax — the military governor Edward Cornwallis issued what became known as the Scalp Proclamation. It offered bounties to anyone who killed a Mi'kmaq person. Men, women, children. The bounty was ten guineas per scalp. A follow-up proclamation in 1756 added prisoners to the list.

[HOST] [calm] This is a fact that Halifax has had to reckon with in recent years — that the man whose statue stood in the centre of the city for over a century, whose name was on a school, whose image was on a plaque — that man had authorized the scalping of the indigenous people of this land. The statue came down in 2018. The reckoning continues.

[HOST] But back in the 1750s, the pressure was building toward something enormous. In 1755, with the Seven Years' War beginning to erupt across two continents, the British Lieutenant Governor of Nova Scotia, Charles Lawrence, decided the Acadian question had to be resolved — permanently.

## SEGMENT 4 — Le Grand Dérangement

[HOST] July 28th, 1755. Charles Lawrence and the Nova Scotia Council issue the deportation orders. What follows is one of the most shattering forced population removals in the history of North America.

[HOST] [calm] The mechanics of it were almost bureaucratic in their cruelty. British soldiers arrived at Acadian communities — Grand Pré, Beaubassin, Pisiquid, Annapolis Royal — and announced that the inhabitants were to assemble at the church, or the meeting house, to hear a proclamation from the governor. Families came. The men and older boys were separated and held. Then they were told: your lands, your livestock, your houses — everything you have built for a hundred and fifty years — are forfeit to the Crown. You are being transported to the British colonies to the south.

[HOST] [sighs] Some families were separated on the dock. Husbands put on one ship, wives and children on another. Some never found each other again. The ships were overcrowded. Disease spread quickly. Of the approximately fourteen thousand Acadians living in Nova Scotia at the time, roughly eleven thousand five hundred were deported. Of those, historians estimate that five thousand died — of starvation, of disease, of shipwrecks — before they reached their destinations.

[HOST] They were scattered up and down the Atlantic coast — Pennsylvania, Maryland, Virginia, the Carolinas, Massachusetts. Some eventually made it to France, only to find that France didn't particularly want them either. Some ended up in Louisiana, where their descendants became the Cajun people, still a living culture today. The name *Cajun* is simply a corruption of the word *Acadian*.

[HOST] [curious] And the Mi'kmaq? The British continued their campaigns against them through the 1750s and 1760s. The scalp bounties remained in effect. Disease did what muskets couldn't always accomplish. By the time the dust settled at the end of the Seven Years' War in 1763, the entire social and cultural landscape of what would become Atlantic Canada had been violently remade.

[HOST] The Acadian villages were burned — not just abandoned, burned — so that no one would be tempted to return. New England settlers, called Planters, were brought in to take up the vacated farms. The dykes the Acadians had built still stood. The fields they had reclaimed from the salt marsh were still there, waiting.

[HOST] But the people were gone.

## OUTRO

[HOST] [paper rustling] Here is the thing about Halifax that I want you to carry into every episode of this series. This city was founded in 1749, just six years before the Expulsion. It was founded specifically as a British military and administrative counter to French power in the region. Its very existence was part of the political calculus that made Le Grand Dérangement possible.

[HOST] Halifax did not cause the Expulsion. But Halifax was built into a world where the Expulsion was being planned, and it became the administrative capital of the colony that carried it out. That is the foundation this city stands on — and if you're going to understand Halifax, you have to understand that.

[HOST] [calm] In the next episode, we go inside the founding of Halifax itself — 1749, Cornwallis and his 2,500 settlers sailing into that harbour, the building of a fortified town from scratch, and why this particular spot on the map became the military and commercial anchor of British North America.
